# Insight 1 — Higher-Income Areas Tend to Have Higher-Priced Restaurants

## Visualization

(Figure: income_vs_price.png)

## Observation

The analysis shows a positive relationship between neighborhood income level and the average restaurant price level.
ZIP codes with higher median household income tend to have restaurants with higher price levels on average.

In the lowest income group, the average price level is approximately 1.7, while in the highest income group it increases to around 1.8.
Although the difference is not extremely large, the trend is consistent across income groups, indicating that higher-income areas support more expensive restaurants.

## Interpretation

This pattern suggests that consumer purchasing power plays an important role in determining the types of restaurants that operate successfully in a given area.
Higher-income neighborhoods likely have customers who are more willing to pay for premium dining experiences, which encourages the presence of higher-priced restaurants.

## Business Insight

This result indicates that:

Premium or fine-dining restaurants are more likely to succeed in high-income areas

Lower-income areas tend to support more budget-friendly dining options

Market entry strategies should consider local income levels when choosing restaurant type and pricing

For restaurant chains or investors, targeting high-income ZIP codes may increase the likelihood of success for higher-priced concepts, while lower-income areas may be better suited for fast-casual or low-cost restaurants.

## Conclusion

Income level is a meaningful predictor of restaurant pricing patterns, and geographic income data can be used as a useful signal when making business decisions about restaurant placement and pricing strategy.

# Insight 2 — Restaurant Type Distribution Differs Across Cities

## Visualization

(Figure: cuisine_by_city.png)

## Observation

The distribution of restaurant-related categories differs across the four cities analyzed: San Francisco, New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago.
Across all cities, the most common categories include bar, store, meal takeaway, cafe, and meal delivery, but the relative counts of these categories vary significantly by location.

New York shows the highest overall counts in all categories, reflecting its larger population and higher density of businesses.
Los Angeles and Chicago also show strong representation of multiple categories, while San Francisco has lower total counts due to its smaller geographic area.

Bars appear to be the most common category in every city, followed by stores and takeaway-related services.
Meal delivery and cafe categories also show strong presence, especially in larger cities.

## Interpretation

The differences in category distribution likely reflect variations in urban density, consumer behavior, and lifestyle patterns.

- Cities with larger populations tend to have higher counts across all categories.
- Higher numbers of meal delivery and takeaway services in New York suggest stronger demand for convenience-based dining.
- The high number of bars and cafes across all cities indicates consistent demand for social and casual dining environments.
- Differences between cities may also reflect local regulations, tourism activity, and cultural preferences.

## Business Insight

These results suggest that restaurant market structure is not uniform across cities, and expansion strategies should consider local category demand.

### Key implications:

- Cities with high counts in delivery and takeaway may be more suitable for fast-casual or delivery-focused businesses.
- High bar and cafe counts indicate strong competition in social dining segments.
- Lower counts in certain categories may indicate opportunities for new restaurants.

### For example:

- New York may be highly competitive but offers strong demand.
- San Francisco may offer smaller but more specialized markets.
- Los Angeles shows balanced demand across multiple categories.
- Chicago shows strong presence of both dining and convenience-oriented services.

## Conclusion

Restaurant category distribution varies substantially across cities, and understanding these differences can help guide decisions about restaurant type, pricing strategy, and market entry location.
Geographic analysis of restaurant categories provides valuable business insight for expansion planning and competitive analysis.

# Insight 3 — Restaurant Density vs Income by City

## Visualization

(Figure: density_vs_income.png)

## Observation

The scatter plot shows the relationship between median household income and restaurant density (restaurants per 1000 people) for four major cities: Chicago, Los Angeles, New York, and San Francisco.

The results indicate that restaurant density does not increase with income.

Chicago has the lowest median household income among the four cities, but it shows the highest restaurant density, with more than 2 restaurants per 1000 people.
San Francisco has the highest income level, yet its restaurant density is lower than Chicago.
Los Angeles and New York have similar income levels and also show very similar restaurant density values, both lower than Chicago.

Overall, the data suggests that income level alone does not explain the number of restaurants per capita in a city.

## Interpretation

Restaurant density appears to be influenced by multiple factors beyond income, including population distribution, city structure, tourism activity, and local dining culture.

Chicago’s high restaurant density may reflect a strong urban dining culture and a high concentration of commercial areas.
San Francisco’s high income but moderate density may be related to limited space, zoning restrictions, or higher operating costs for restaurants.
Los Angeles and New York have large populations and wide geographic areas, which may reduce the number of restaurants per capita when averaged across the entire city.

These results show that economic wealth does not directly determine restaurant availability.

## Business Insight

For restaurant expansion planning, income level should not be used as the only indicator of market opportunity.

### Key implications:

- High-income cities do not always have more restaurants per person.
- Lower-income cities may still support a high number of restaurants.
- Market saturation depends on population density and urban structure.
- Restaurant businesses should analyze both demographic and geographic factors before choosing new locations.

Using restaurant density together with income provides a more accurate understanding of market competition.

## Conclusion

Restaurant density varies significantly across cities and does not show a clear positive relationship with income level.
Understanding both economic and spatial factors is important when evaluating restaurant markets in different urban areas.

# Insight 4 — Average Rating by Price Level

## Visualization

(Figure: price_vs_rating.png)

## Observation

The bar chart shows the average restaurant rating for different price levels, ranging from 1 (low price) to 4 (high price).

The results indicate a clear upward trend in ratings as price level increases.
Restaurants with price level 1 have the lowest average rating, slightly above 4.2.
As the price level increases to 2 and 3, the average rating also increases gradually.
Restaurants with price level 4 have the highest average rating, close to 4.5.

Although the differences are not extremely large, the pattern is consistent across all price categories.

## Interpretation

Higher-priced restaurants tend to receive slightly higher ratings.
This may reflect differences in service quality, food quality, atmosphere, or customer expectations.

More expensive restaurants often provide:

- better ingredients
- more professional service
- more comfortable environments
- more specialized cuisin

These factors can lead to higher customer satisfaction, which is reflected in the ratings.

However, the rating differences are relatively small, suggesting that lower-priced restaurants can still achieve high ratings if they provide good value.

## Business Insight

Price level alone does not determine success, but there is a positive relationship between price and average rating.

### Key implications:

- Higher-priced restaurants tend to receive slightly better reviews.
- Affordable restaurants can still compete if quality is high.
- Customers may have higher expectations for expensive restaurants.
- Pricing strategy should balance quality, cost, and customer experience.

Restaurant owners should consider both price positioning and service quality when trying to improve ratings.

## Conclusion

Average restaurant rating increases slightly with price level, suggesting a positive relationship between cost and perceived quality.
However, the difference is moderate, indicating that both low-price and high-price restaurants can achieve strong customer satisfaction.

# Insight 5 — Rating vs Review Count

## Visualization

(Figure: rating_vs_reviews.png)

## Observation

The scatter plot shows the relationship between the number of user reviews and the average rating for restaurants.

Most restaurants have ratings between 4.0 and 4.7, regardless of the number of reviews.
However, restaurants with very few reviews show a wider range of ratings, including very high and very low values.
As the number of reviews increases, the ratings tend to concentrate around the middle range.

The trend line shows a slight downward slope, indicating that restaurants with more reviews tend to have slightly lower average ratings.

## Interpretation

Restaurants with a small number of reviews often have more extreme ratings because the sample size is small.
A few positive or negative reviews can significantly affect the average score.

Restaurants with many reviews have more stable ratings, since the average is based on a larger number of opinions.
This makes the rating more reliable and closer to the true customer experience.

The slight downward trend suggests that highly reviewed restaurants may receive more balanced feedback over time.

## Business Insight

The number of reviews should be considered together with rating when evaluating restaurant quality.

### Key implications:

- High rating with few reviews may not be reliable.
- Restaurants with many reviews provide more trustworthy ratings.
- Review count is an important indicator of popularity and credibility.
- Businesses should focus on both customer satisfaction and review volume.

Customers and analysts should not rely on rating alone when comparing restaurants.

## Conclusion

Ratings remain high across most restaurants, but review count affects rating stability.
Restaurants with more reviews tend to have more consistent ratings, while restaurants with fewer reviews show greater variation.
